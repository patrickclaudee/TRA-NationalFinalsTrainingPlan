Do nothing new. No new shoes, no new food, no heroics. Sleep early, eat what you know, hydrate from Thursday. The work is done. Trust it.

RACE DAY STRATEGY — SUN NOV 8

Km 0–10: 5:50–5:55/km. Resist the crowd. Run by feel — slower than target pace. Your ego will want to go faster. Ignore it.
Km 10–21: Lock into 5:41–5:45/km. Cruise zone. Controlled — not comfortable, not hard. Gel every 6km, salt at 10km.
Km 21–32: This is where the race starts. Maintain. Push 5:35 if feeling good at 25km. Caffeine gel at 22km.
Km 32–42: This section ended your first marathon. You've trained for this. Sub-4:00 is yours if you hit 32km under 3:03. Empty the tank.

Nutrition: Carbs/Gel every 6km. Salt at 10km & 20km. Caffeine gel at 22km.
Race Shoe: 361 Flame 5  ·  Sub-4:00 = 5:41/km. Memorize it.

Key Principles

MP In Long Runs

Unlike Leg 3, long runs here have marathon pace embedded. Your legs need to know 5:41/km at km 20+. That contrast is what wins the second half.

Easy Means Easy

Easy portions in long runs must be 6:30–6:45/km. Run them at 6:00 and the MP segments fall apart. The contrast is where adaptation lives.

5:41/km Is Your Anchor

Every marathon pace segment trains this number. By race day it should feel familiar — almost boring. If it feels hard on Nov 8, you went out too fast.

The km 37 Problem

Your first marathon failed muscularly, not cardiovascularly. 32–35km training runs at race effort mean km 37 won't be new territory in November.

Week 4 Is Sacred

Don't compress or skip the cutback week. Weeks 5–6 at 95km are only productive if week 4 lets the adaptation from weeks 2–3 settle in properly.

Taper Is Intentional

Legs feeling flat and restless from week 7 onward is correct. That's glycogen supercompensation and nervous system recovery. Don't add junk miles to "feel ready."
